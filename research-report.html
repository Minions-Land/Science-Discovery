A field report · May 2026

AI Agents, Skills, and the Academic Workflow

Somewhere between the 2024 prompt-engineering era and the late-2026 papers where agents write their own papers, a new object quietly took over scientific tooling: the skill. This is a tour of where that object came from, what it can do today, how we measure it, and one paper that might change the shape of scholarly publishing entirely.

By the editors · Reading time ~14 min · 2026-05-11

IA hinge point

Two years that changed what "using AI to do research" means.

In August 2024, a system called AI Scientist submitted a paper to a real machine-learning workshop and got it accepted. Nobody on the program committee was told, at first, that it had been written end-to-end by an autonomous agent. The paper was not a masterpiece, but it was plausible: it proposed a hypothesis, ran experiments, plotted results, and assembled a draft. A year and a half later, in January 2026, Nature published the technical follow-up, making it the first time a journal of that caliber treated "the researcher was an agent" as a first-class methodological fact.

Two months before that publication, OpenAI had quietly shipped PaperBench: a benchmark in which an agent must reproduce twenty ICML-2024 Spotlight or Oral papers from scratch, graded against 8,316 fine-grained rubric leaves that the original authors helped design. And in April 2026, a 38-author team from MIT Media Lab, Michigan, and Stanford posted a proposal to replace the paper itself — not the writing of papers, but the object of a paper — with something more amenable to machines. We will come back to that paper at length in Section V.

These are not isolated events. They sit on top of a much quieter change that has happened in developer tooling over roughly eighteen months: the emergence of the skill as the basic unit of agent capability. A skill is not a model, not a prompt, not a plugin in the 2023 sense; it is a small, self-contained procedure that an AI agent can discover, read, and invoke on its own. There are now tens of thousands of them. They are, cumulatively, what makes the PaperBench and AI-Scientist stories possible — and they are what the rest of this essay is about.

IIWhat a skill is, actually

Before the survey, a definition — because the word is often used loosely.

An agent skill is a folder on disk. Inside that folder is a single Markdown file, usually called SKILL.md, with a short YAML header that states the skill's name, what it is for, and the conditions under which an agent should reach for it. Below the header is prose: a procedure, a checklist, sometimes a small script, always written to be read by a language model rather than a human. Supporting material — reference pages, templates, helper code — sits beside it in optional sub-folders.

That sounds modest, and it is. The point is what the modesty buys. A skill is discoverable — the agent finds it without being told. It is composable — one skill can call another without coordination from a human. It is portable — the same folder works in Claude Code, Cursor, Codex, or Gemini CLI because they have converged on the same on-disk format. And it is legible — a PhD student can read the Markdown and understand what the agent is supposed to do, which is the precondition for trusting the agent to do it.

Compared with a prompt, a skill is durable: it lives in a file rather than a chat window, which means it can be versioned, reviewed, and cited. Compared with a plugin in the 2023 ChatGPT sense, it is cheap: no API registration, no OAuth dance, no publisher review. The barrier between "I have a procedure that works" and "my agent now knows this procedure" has collapsed to the act of writing a Markdown file. That collapse is why the ecosystem grew so fast.

IIIThe ecosystem, seen from 30,000 feet

Who builds skills, where they live, and how to read the topology.

If you search GitHub for repositories tagged claude-skills or agent-skills, you will find the current ecosystem resolves into four distinct layers. It is worth naming them, because they have different purposes, different audiences, and very different half-lives.

1. The reference implementations

At the bottom sit the model-lab repositories — anthropics/skills (131k stars) and the corresponding official plugin directory. These set the format, settle debates about the YAML schema, and ship the document-processing primitives (PDF, DOCX, XLSX, PPTX) that almost every downstream skill relies on.

2. The domain collections

Above them sit focused collections built by practitioners — labs, journals, and individual researchers who codify their own methodology. In academia, the most notable are:

The cadence at which this layer is moving is perhaps the single clearest signal that the field has entered a new phase. academic-research-skills, the four-skill pipeline in the table above, is a useful case in point: opened on 26 February 2026 by the independent developer Cheng-I Wu (吴政宜), it crossed 6,000 stars in roughly seventy-five days, trending on Hacker News along the way, and shipped its seventh major release (v3.7.0) in early May. Ten years ago, a solo author shipping methodology of this quality would have needed a tenured appointment and a graduate student to maintain it. The distribution graph has quietly flattened: a weekend, a SKILL.md, and a readable repository are now enough for a piece of research infrastructure to acquire real users.

3. The autonomous research systems

A parallel thread does not ship skills at all — it ships entire pipelines. The best-known are Sakana's AI Scientist (13.5k ⭐) and its v2, gpt-researcher (27k ⭐), Agent Laboratory, and HKU's AI-Researcher (NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight). These are the systems that take a research question and return a draft paper — and whose numbers set the ceiling against which the skill-based approach must eventually be measured.

4. The meta-lists

Finally, a small number of curated catalogues track the whole landscape so you don't have to. Two are worth knowing: the community-run ComposioHQ / awesome-claude-skills (59k ⭐), which is the most comprehensive general-purpose index, and the Stanford REAP × CoPaper.AI list, which specialises in empirical social-science workflows and, as of its April 2026 update, indexes 119 repositories covering 23,000+ skills, organised by research stage.

IVMeasuring progress

Twenty-nine public benchmarks, organised by the stage of research they evaluate.

A healthy field needs public benchmarks. For scientific agents, the last two years produced an unusually clean set of them — not because any single one is perfect, but because together they cover the whole research pipeline with enough overlap to triangulate. What follows is a condensed map. Every row has been cross-checked against OpenAlex for authorship and DOI; links go to the canonical preprint where one exists.

Browsing and general assistance

The earliest and best-known is GAIA (Mialon et al., 2023) — 450 real-world questions in three difficulty tiers. Humans score 92%; GPT-4 with plug-ins at release scored about 15%. GAIA is still the default entry point for anyone evaluating a research-assistant agent. Its successor papers are BrowseComp (OpenAI, 2025, 1,266 hard-to-find-information questions) and SimpleQA (OpenAI, 2024, 4,326 short-form factuality questions designed to surface hallucination). Humanity's Last Exam (CAIS + Scale, 2025) raises the ceiling with 3,000+ expert-grade questions across the sciences and humanities.

Machine-learning research as a task

Here the canonical three are MLAgentBench (Huang et al., ICML 2024: 13 Kaggle-style improvement tasks), MLE-Bench (OpenAI, 2024: 75 Kaggle competitions, scored by medal rate against human percentile), and RE-Bench (METR, 2024: seven AI-R&D tasks where the baseline is not a model but a human researcher on the same four-hour budget). Alongside them: ScienceAgentBench (OSU, ICLR'25: 102 scientific data-analysis tasks), SUPER (AI2, EMNLP 2024: reproducing research repositories), and CORE-Bench (2024: 90 computational reproducibility tasks).

Paper-level reproduction

This category is the bar for end-to-end competence. The dominant entry is PaperBench: given an ICML 2024 paper and its appendix, reproduce the work from scratch. Scoring is done against a rubric tree that the original authors helped design and that decomposes each paper into 8,316 evaluable leaves. It is the most honest benchmark in this space because the rubric was built by the people who know what the paper was supposed to prove. Paper2Code (2025) is a lighter-weight cousin focused on paper-to-runnable-code.

Literature review, idea generation, and peer review

The survey-writing task has a clear leader: AutoSurvey (NeurIPS 2024). Its four-dimensional rubric — citation recall, citation precision, content coverage, structural coherence — has quietly become the de facto metric for long-form scholarly generation.

For idea generation, the reference is a Stanford human study from NeurIPS 2024 (Si et al.), which asked 79 NLP researchers to blindly rate AI-generated vs. human-generated research ideas on novelty, excitement, feasibility, and effectiveness. The finding — that AI ideas score higher on novelty but lower on feasibility — is the closest thing the field has to a load-bearing empirical result. IdeaBench and AAAR-1.0 automate pieces of that.

For peer review, the historical anchor is PeerRead (NAACL 2018), still cited; the modern entries are ReviewerGPT (CMU, 2023), Reviewer2 (2024), and AgentReview (EMNLP 2024), which is particularly interesting because it introduces deliberately biased reviewers to test whether the multi-agent simulation reproduces the known pathologies of real conference review.

Figures and tables

ChartQA (ACL 2022) is still the canon for chart understanding; SciCap+ and Paper2Fig100k handle the caption-generation and figure-reconstruction tasks respectively.

VThe frontier: Agent-Native Research Artifacts

One paper from April 2026 that, if correct, changes what "a paper" is.

In late April 2026 a team of 38 authors — led from MIT Media Lab, the University of Michigan, and Stanford, and including Alex Pentland — posted an arXiv preprint with the deliberately provocative title The Last Human-Written Paper: Agent-Native Research Artifacts. The claim is not that humans will stop writing papers. The claim is that the paper itself — the PDF, the narrative, the compressed linear argument — has been the wrong object for about two years, and nobody noticed because only humans were reading them.

Paper: arXiv:2604.24658 · Code: Orchestra-Research/Agent-Native-Research-Artifact · License: MIT

The diagnosis: two taxes

The authors' framing is precise. Publication compiles a branching, iterative research process into a linear narrative, and the compilation imposes two structural costs.

The storytelling tax is what the paper throws away to fit the genre — failed experiments, rejected hypotheses, the branching exploration process that actually produced the finding. These are discarded because they do not fit a linear argument, and their absence is invisible to a reader who already trusts the author.

The engineering tax is the gap between "enough prose for a reviewer" and "enough specification for a machine to reproduce the work". Authors write to a reader who can infer missing detail; agents cannot infer, so they rediscover every missing detail at cost.

The empirical case, in two numbers Only 45.4% of the 8,921 reproduction requirements extracted from 23 ICML 2024 papers are fully specified in the published PDFs. And on RE-Bench, failed agent runs account for 90.2% of total dollar cost across 24,008 runs, because each agent independently rediscovers the same dead ends. Tolerable when every reader was human; load-bearing waste when readers are machines.

The proposal: a four-layer artifact

In place of the PDF the authors propose an Agent-Native Research Artifact (ARA) — a directory structured in four interlocking layers, meant to be read and written by agents, though still legible to humans.

Three details make the protocol more than a filesystem convention. First, every claim in logic/ carries a forensic binding to the experiment that supports it, and every experiment binds to raw evidence in evidence/. Second, every entry carries a provenance tag — user, ai-suggested, ai-executed, or user-revised — distinguishing human-confirmed facts from AI inferences. Third, and most distinctively, dead ends are first-class citizens: the exploration tree preserves rejected alternatives and failed approaches alongside the surviving story, so future agents do not have to rediscover them.

A twenty-one-point jump on PaperBench question-answering is not subtle. It is the kind of number that usually means either the benchmark is broken or the intervention is real. The PaperBench rubric is, as discussed, unusually author-validated, which makes the second reading hard to dismiss.

The authors are refreshingly honest about the limit: on RE-Bench's five open-ended extension tasks, preserved failure traces can sometimes constrain a capable agent — locking it inside the box the previous run already explored, rather than letting it step outside. The benefit is strongly conditional on the agent's own strength. That caveat, placed in the abstract rather than buried in the appendix, tells you something about the paper's seriousness.

VIThree open questions

Work that looks doable right now, for whoever wants to pick it up.

An essay that ends "and now the future is bright" is not worth reading. Here, instead, are three concrete questions we think the next twelve months will settle — and where the evidence is still thin enough that small teams can make a real contribution.

Does composition beat specialisation?

Every serious skill collection is built by one author with one style. But nothing prevents a citation skill from one author from being called by a drafting skill from another, as long as both follow the SKILL.md convention. Nobody has measured, with rigour, whether a composed stack outperforms any single monolithic collection on an end-to-end benchmark like PaperBench. This is the cheapest real experiment in the field right now.

Do English benchmarks transfer to other languages?

Every benchmark in Section IV is English-first. GAIA, PaperBench, AutoSurvey, SimpleQA — all of them. There are real reasons to suspect the gap is larger than the language itself: the citation ecology (CNKI and Wanfang rather than Semantic Scholar), the rhetorical conventions of Chinese or Japanese academic prose, the figure caption norms of national journals. A small Chinese-AutoSurvey benchmark, built against real CNKI reference pools rather than GPT-fabricated ones, would be a genuine contribution — and there is no prestigious group obviously working on it yet.

How durable is the ARA protocol?

Section V presented ARA in its best light. The open empirical question is whether the twenty-one-point PaperBench gain survives when you change the backbone model, the agent scaffolding, or the domain. If the gain is robust across all three, ARA is the real thing. If it is not, the intervention is model-specific and the field will keep looking. Either answer is publishable.
