## RED
**Polished Abstract:**

Precise genome editing holds promise for treating neurological disease, but delivering CRISPR-Cas9 to the central nervous system remains challenging due to the blood-brain barrier and safety concerns with viral vectors. We engineered a lipid nanoparticle formulation containing the ionisable lipid 14B and a brain-targeting peptide to overcome these barriers. Intravenous injection of LNPs carrying Cas9 mRNA and a guide RNA targeting PCSK9 achieved genome editing in 38% of cortical neurons in mice 14 days post-injection, with no adverse effects observed. This editing efficiency substantially exceeds prior non-viral CNS delivery approaches and demonstrates that rationally designed LNPs can achieve functional gene editing in the brain. Our results establish a non-viral platform for CNS genome editing applicable to neurological disease models and potentially to therapeutic development.

**Three-bullet summary of changes:**

- Removed method clutter: deleted microfluidic flow ratio, particle size, PDI, encapsulation efficiency.
- Strengthened significance: replaced "support further evaluation" with "establishes a platform"; closed with "applicable to neurological disease models and potentially to therapeutic development."

## BLUE
**Polished Abstract:**

Precise genome editing via CRISPR-Cas9 offers therapeutic potential across neurological diseases, yet delivery to the central nervous system remains constrained by the blood-brain barrier and safety concerns with viral vectors. Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) provide a non-viral alternative, but CNS-targeted formulations with high encapsulation efficiency remain underdeveloped. Here, we show that an LNP formulation containing ionisable lipid 14B and a brain-targeting peptide, produced by microfluidic mixing, achieves 92% mRNA encapsulation and delivers functional Cas9 to cortical neurons in mice. Intravenous injection of LNPs carrying Cas9 mRNA and a PCSK9-targeting guide RNA produced genome editing in 38% of cortical neurons 14 days post-injection, with no adverse effects in the tested cohort. These results establish a non-viral route for CNS genome editing in mice and define a platform for further preclinical evaluation in neurological disease models.

**Three changes:**
- Replaced narrative "We developed" with direct "Here, we show."
- Bounded the closing implication to tested scope — establishes a non-viral route in mice and defines a platform for further preclinical evaluation.
- Restructured for the six-rung ladder.

## GREEN
**Polished Abstract:**

Precise genome editing via CRISPR-Cas9 offers therapeutic potential for neurological disease, yet delivery across the blood-brain barrier remains a major barrier to clinical translation. Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) provide a non-viral alternative to viral vectors, but CNS-targeted formulations with high encapsulation efficiency and neuronal penetration are lacking. Here, we show that an LNP formulation containing the ionisable lipid 14B and a brain-targeting peptide, produced by microfluidic mixing, achieves 92% mRNA encapsulation and 78 nm particle size (PDI 0.12). Intravenous delivery of Cas9 mRNA and PCSK9-targeting guide RNA produced genome editing in 38% of cortical neurons in mice 14 days post-injection, with no adverse effects observed. These results establish a non-viral route for CNS genome editing in mice and define a platform for further preclinical evaluation in neurological disease models.

**Three changes:**
- Rung 4 anchor: replaced "We developed" with "Here, we show."
- Rung 6 boundary: changed "supports further evaluation" to "establishes a non-viral route ... and defines a platform" — claims only what mouse data demonstrate.
- Rung 2 tightening: reframed LNP background as a solved-problem gap.
