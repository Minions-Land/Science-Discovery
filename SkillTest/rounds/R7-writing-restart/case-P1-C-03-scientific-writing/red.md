Cell segmentation in fluorescence microscopy remains a critical bottleneck for quantitative biology. While supervised methods such as U-Net and Cellpose have achieved widespread adoption, they require approximately 10,000 annotated images per cell type, making them impractical for emerging microscopy modalities. Here, we introduce ContrastiveCellSeg, a self-supervised pretraining approach that substantially reduces annotation requirements. Our method employs SimCLR-style contrastive learning on 2.3 million unlabeled patches from the LIVECell and CellPose-Cyto2 datasets, using a ResNet-50 backbone trained for 200 epochs. Following pretraining, fine-tuning on as few as 100 labeled cells achieves a mean average precision of 0.847 at IoU threshold 0.5 on the held-out LIVECell test split, representing a 16% relative improvement over U-Net (0.731) and outperforming fine-tuned Cellpose (0.802). These results demonstrate that contrastive pretraining can substantially reduce the annotation burden for fluorescence-microscopy segmentation, with broad applications across cell biology, drug discovery, and clinical pathology.
