Abstract

Quantum computing represents a paradigm shift in computational technology, leveraging the principles of quantum mechanics to process information in fundamentally new ways. Unlike classical computers that use bits representing either 0 or 1, quantum computers employ quantum bits (qubits) that can exist in superposition states, enabling parallel processing of multiple computational paths simultaneously. This paper explores the current state of quantum computing technology, examining key developments in qubit implementation, error correction mechanisms, and quantum algorithms. We analyze the potential applications across cryptography, drug discovery, optimization problems, and materials science, while addressing the significant challenges that remain, including decoherence, scalability, and the need for extremely low operating temperatures. Our findings suggest that while practical quantum advantage has been demonstrated in specific domains, widespread commercial viability remains several years away. The research highlights both the transformative potential of quantum computing and the substantial technical hurdles that must be overcome to realize its full promise.
