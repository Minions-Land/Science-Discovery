Protein structure prediction has undergone a fundamental transformation since the introduction of AlphaFold-2 and its successors. These deep-learning systems achieve remarkable accuracy on natural sequences and have accelerated structural biology across multiple domains. However, adapting these models to new design tasks—such as predicting novel folds or engineering de novo binders—remains computationally expensive. Retraining or fine-tuning AlphaFold-2 for such applications typically requires tens of thousands of GPU-hours, creating a practical barrier for many research groups and limiting the pace of protein engineering.

Two complementary directions have emerged to address this bottleneck. Diffusion-based generative models have shown promise as learned priors over protein backbone structures, capturing the distribution of natural folds without requiring task-specific retraining. Simultaneously, retrieval-augmented approaches have demonstrated that conditioning language models on structurally similar examples from curated databases can improve prediction accuracy. Yet neither approach alone has matched AlphaFold-2's performance on challenging design tasks, suggesting that their combination might unlock new capabilities.

Here we introduce RetroDiff, a method that integrates a diffusion prior over protein backbones with retrieval-augmented decoding. The diffusion prior is a learned denoising model trained on a large corpus of natural protein structures. The retrieval module identifies structurally similar examples from a curated subset of the Protein Data Bank and uses these examples to condition the generation process at each step of inference. By combining these two components, RetroDiff achieves competitive accuracy on challenging structure-prediction benchmarks while requiring substantially less computational resources than retraining existing models.

On CASP15 free-modelling targets, RetroDiff achieves a TM-score of 0.71, comparable to AlphaFold-2's 0.73, while reducing inference computational cost by 30-fold. This efficiency gain makes structure prediction more accessible and enables rapid iteration in protein design workflows. We demonstrate that the method generalizes across diverse protein families and remains robust when applied to sequences with limited homology information.
