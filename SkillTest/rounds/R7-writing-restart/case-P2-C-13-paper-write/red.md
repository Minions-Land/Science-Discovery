Protein structure prediction has undergone a transformation in recent years, driven by deep learning approaches that combine evolutionary information with learned priors over three-dimensional geometry. AlphaFold-2 and its successors have achieved remarkable accuracy on structure prediction tasks, establishing new benchmarks for the field. However, these advances have come at substantial computational cost: adapting or retraining such models for new design tasks—such as predicting novel folds or designing de novo binders—remains prohibitively expensive, often requiring tens of thousands of GPU-hours per optimization run.

Two complementary research directions have emerged to address this limitation. First, diffusion-based generative models have shown promise as learned priors over protein backbone structures, offering a flexible framework for sampling diverse conformations. Second, retrieval-augmented approaches have demonstrated that conditioning language models on similar examples from curated databases can improve prediction accuracy without full retraining. Each approach alone, however, falls short of matching AlphaFold-2 accuracy on challenging design tasks, suggesting that their combination might unlock new capabilities.

Here we introduce RetroDiff, a method that combines a diffusion-based structural prior with retrieval-augmented decoding to achieve competitive accuracy on protein structure prediction while dramatically reducing computational requirements. The approach trains a denoising score model as a learned prior over protein backbones, then augments inference with a retrieval module that conditions each diffusion step on structurally similar examples from a curated subset of the Protein Data Bank. On CASP15 free-modelling targets, RetroDiff achieves a TM-score of 0.71—comparable to AlphaFold-2's 0.73—while requiring 30-fold less GPU time at inference. This efficiency gain opens new possibilities for rapid structure-based design workflows and makes high-accuracy prediction accessible to resource-constrained settings.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. We first review related work in diffusion-based structure generation and retrieval-augmented inference. We then describe the RetroDiff architecture, including the diffusion prior, retrieval module, and inference procedure. We present experimental results on CASP15 targets and additional benchmarks, including ablations that isolate the contribution of each component. Finally, we discuss limitations and directions for future work.
